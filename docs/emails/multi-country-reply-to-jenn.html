What Is a Persona (and Why It Matters)

Before the breakdown, quick context on how personas work because they drive everything downstream.

A persona is a real-person representation of who qualifies for this specific job. It's not a generic marketing avatar — it's derived directly from the job requirements the recruiter submits in the intake form. The intake captures 7 structured fields:

  • Required qualifications — credentials, degrees, certifications, years of experience
  • Preferred qualifications — nice-to-haves that strengthen an application
  • Location scope — geographic requirements and exclusions
  • Language requirements — languages, proficiency levels, modality notes
  • Engagement model — duration, commitment, compensation structure
  • Equipment & tools — hardware, software, reference material access
  • Context notes — creative-team brief on the project's real audience and tone

From these, the AI derives persona constraints: what the minimum credentials are, which career stages / tiers are acceptable, what age range fits, and which archetypes are explicitly excluded (so we never target people who don't qualify). It then generates 3 distinct personas — each matching a different acceptable tier from the job requirements, grounded in the cultural research for that country.

Example: If the job requires "licensed dermatologist OR dermatology resident OR clinical-year medical student with documented dermatology rotation," the system generates 3 personas: a practicing dermatologist, a second-year resident, and a clinical-year student. Each gets different messaging (the resident cares about supplemental income during training; the practicing physician cares about shaping AI clinical tools). The system will NOT generate a generic "gig worker" persona because that archetype is excluded by the qualification constraints.

Every piece of content downstream — images, copy, creatives — is built for a specific persona. This is why the ads feel targeted instead of generic: the copy speaks to a resident's specific frustrations, not "join our platform."

Why We Generate This Much

Quick context on the logic before I recommend cuts. The volume isn't there to overwhelm reviewers — it's there to give the ad platform enough variations to optimize against. When you run paid ads, the platform (Meta, Google, LinkedIn) tests different combinations of headlines, images, and descriptions to find what converts best. If you give it 1 creative, it runs that. If you give it 15 variations, it finds the winner in the first 48 hours and shifts budget to it automatically.

The goal isn't for Miguel to review 2,100 items. It's for Miguel to approve ~40 items, and the ad platform to optimize across them.

On Language

Your point about English readability for SOWs is important. Here's how I'd approach it:

  • All internal-facing content stays in English — briefs, strategies, review comments, approval notes. Nothing changes there.
  • Ad copy is where language matters. The contributor sees the ad in their feed. If it's in their language, CTR goes up — but only in markets where that's true. Some markets (Malaysia, South Africa, anglophone countries) perform fine in English.
  • My recommendation: start with English everywhere, then promote countries to local-language as we get CTR data. We can flip a country from Tier 3 to Tier 1 in the system with one setting change. This way we're not guessing — we run English first, measure, and localize where the data says it matters.

This also sidesteps the platform translation question entirely. Ad creative language is independent of whether OneForma's interface is translated.

On Cost vs. Return

Agreed that we need to track this. The tiered system actually makes this straightforward:

  • Each country has a known contributor rate (from the rate sheet)
  • Each country has a media strategy with recommended ad spend
  • We track ad performance per country (CTR, CPA, completions)
  • ROAS = (Completions × Fulfillment Rate × Net Rate) / Ad Spend

Countries that don't justify their ad spend get demoted or cut from future campaigns. Countries that outperform get promoted to Tier 1 for full localization. The system learns over time which markets are worth the investment.
